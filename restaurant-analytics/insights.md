# Restaurant Performance Analysis — Jul–Dec 2024

## Executive Summary

The restaurant brought in **R$ 1,467,750.10** over six months, averaging around R$ 244,600/month. Revenue was stable throughout (no significant drops, no sharp peaks)which points to a consistent operation. August was the strongest month (R$ 263K) and July the weakest (R$ 227K), though the gap is only 16% and July marks the start of school holidays in Brazil, a period that typically keeps dining-out demand steady.

The more interesting story isn't in the revenue totals. It's in what happens when you look at margin alongside volume and at what's quietly bleeding money in the waste log.

---

## 1. Revenue Trends

Monthly revenue stayed between R$ 227K and R$ 263K with no downward trend across the period. The numbers are consistent enough that the business doesn't appear to have a demand problem. What the revenue view alone can't tell you is whether the right items are being sold, or whether that R$ 244K average could be higher with the same customer volume.

---

## 2. Sales Volume by Category

Beverages dominate. The five best-selling items are all drinks:

| Item | Units Sold |
|:---|---:|
| Suco Natural | 3,045 |
| Caipirinha | 2,989 |
| Cerveja Artesanal | 2,979 |

Main dishes come in around 1,300–1,400 units per item. Desserts sit at the bottom, 600–700 units each. That's not unusual for Brazilian restaurants — beverages get reordered throughout the meal, which inflates their volume naturally.

The question worth asking is whether high beverage volume is translating into proportional profit, or just into high counts.

---

## 3. Profitability vs. Volume

It doesn't. Beverages lead in units sold but carry lower absolute margins than most main dishes. A Caipirinha at R$ 22.90 with a R$ 4.00 food cost leaves R$ 18.90 margin per unit. A Filé Mignon at R$ 89.90 with a R$ 28.50 cost leaves R$ 61.40. The volume gap between the two doesn't close that difference.

This is the central tension in menu analysis: what sells most isn't necessarily what makes the most money. Revenue ranking and profit ranking produce very different lists, and decisions based on one without the other will land wrong.

---

## 4. Menu Engineering Matrix

The Menu Engineering framework classifies items on two axes — popularity (units sold vs. menu average) and margin (unit margin vs. menu average) — producing four categories:

| Class | Definition | Strategic Action |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Star** | High popularity + high margin | Protect and promote |
| **Plowhorse** | High popularity + low margin | Review pricing or reduce cost |
| **Puzzle** | Low popularity + high margin | Improve visibility |
| **Dog** | Low popularity + low margin | Evaluate removal |

The results show a mix of Stars and Plowhorses, with some Puzzles sitting on high margins that aren't being captured because the items aren't moving. Plowhorses are the most actionable group: they already have the volume, so even a small pricing adjustment or cost reduction has an outsized effect on total margin. A R$ 3–5 price increase on a Plowhorse selling 1,400 units over six months is R$ 4,200–7,000 in additional margin without changing a single customer interaction.

---

## 5. Food Waste Analysis

Total waste cost is heavily concentrated in three ingredients:

| Ingredient | Total Waste Cost |
|:---|---:|
| Camarão (shrimp) | R$ 6,370.18 |
| Carne Bovina (beef) | R$ 2,172.74 |
| Frango (chicken) | R$ 986.03 |

Shrimp stands out not just because of its unit cost (R$ 89/kg) but because it also has one of the highest discarded volumes in the dataset — around 71kg over the period. That's a compounding problem: expensive ingredient, wasted in large quantities. The financial gap between shrimp and everything else in the waste log is significant.

The breakdown by reason points to two separate issues. Expiration (*vencimento*) suggests purchasing frequency or storage is off. Overproduction (*preparo excessivo*) suggests batch sizing isn't calibrated to actual demand. These require different fixes — you can't solve an expiration problem by adjusting portion sizes, and you can't solve an overproduction problem by ordering less frequently.

A 20% reduction in shrimp waste alone would recover around R$ 1,274 over a six-month period. That's not a large number in isolation, but it comes at zero cost to revenue and signals a broader inventory control gap worth addressing.

---

## 6. Recommendations

**On the menu:**
- Protect Star items: keep them visible and well-positioned
- Run a pricing review on Plowhorses before assuming they need a cost reduction; often the margin gap is a pricing issue, not a cost one
- Test Puzzle items with better menu placement or pairing suggestions before writing them off
- Don't remove Dog items reactively, set a formal review cycle with clear thresholds

**On waste:**
- Shrimp and beef are the priority; small improvements there outweigh large improvements anywhere else in the waste log
- Separate the expiration problem (purchasing/storage) from the overproduction problem (batch planning), they're not the same issue and don't have the same solution
- Weekly waste tracking by reason turns this from a reporting exercise into something that actually changes purchasing and production behavior

*Data covers July 1 – December 31, 2024. All monetary values in BRL.*
